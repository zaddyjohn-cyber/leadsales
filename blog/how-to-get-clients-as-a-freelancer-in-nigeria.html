How to Get Clients as a Freelancer in Nigeria Without Upwork or Fiverr (2026)

July 2026 · 8 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

If you are a Nigerian freelancer, you already know the feeling. You open Upwork, you find a good job post — a web design project, a content writing gig, an SEO brief — and you hit "View Proposals." There are already 47 proposals. Sometimes 80. Sometimes over 200. And the client's budget? $30 for something that should cost $500.

This is not a you problem. This is a structural problem with how platforms like Upwork and Fiverr work. They are marketplaces — which means you are always competing, always on display, always one algorithm change away from losing visibility. The platform owns the client. You own nothing.

There is a better way. And it starts with one shift in thinking: instead of waiting for clients to come to you, you go directly to them.

Why Most Nigerian Freelancers Stay Stuck on Upwork

It is not because Upwork is bad. It is because Upwork is the only strategy most people know. Nobody teaches you direct outreach in school. Nobody tells you that there are millions of small businesses in the US, UK, Canada, and Australia sitting on Google Maps right now — with email addresses, phone numbers, and websites that have not been touched since 2018 — just waiting for someone to pitch them.

The businesses are already there. They just do not know you exist.

💡 A single Upwork job post gets an average of 20–50 proposals within hours. A cold email to a business owner gets zero — because nobody else is doing it. That is your advantage.

The Direct Outreach Model: How It Works

Direct outreach means identifying businesses that need your service and emailing or messaging them directly — before they even know they need to post a job. Here is the simple version of how it works:

  1. Pick a niche. Do not email every type of business. Pick one: dentists, real estate agents, restaurants, gyms, law firms, beauty salons. Go deep on one industry before expanding.
  2. Pick a location. The US, UK, and Canada are the highest-paying markets. Within the US, you can target specific states — Texas, Florida, California, New York — to stay focused.
  3. Get a list of businesses with emails. This is the step most people skip because they do not know how to do it. We will cover it below.
  4. Send personalised cold emails. Not spam. Short, specific, relevant emails that show you understand their business and can solve a real problem.
  5. Follow up. Most replies come after the 2nd or 3rd email. One email is not enough.

Step 1: Choosing the Right Niche in Nigeria's Current Market

Some niches work better than others for Nigerian freelancers pitching abroad. Here are the highest-converting ones in 2026:

Web Design / Development

Businesses without websites or with outdated sites are everywhere. In the US alone, over 30% of small businesses still have no functional website. This is a massive opportunity. A simple 5-page website pitch can close at $300–$800 per client.

SEO

Local businesses are desperate for Google rankings. A dentist in Texas who ranks #1 on Google for "dentist near me" gets 10× more calls than one on page 2. If you can do local SEO, there are thousands of clients ready to pay $200–$500/month as a retainer.

Social Media Management

Restaurant owners, gym owners, salon owners — they all know they need Instagram content. Almost none of them have time to do it. Package this at ₦50,000/month minimum or $100–$200/month for overseas clients.

Copywriting / Content Writing

Every business that sells something needs words. Blog posts, product descriptions, email sequences, landing pages. If you can write clearly in English, this is one of the easiest niches to break into.

Video Editing

With short-form video dominating every platform, coaches, consultants, and brands are drowning in raw footage they do not have time to edit. Fast turnaround video editors are in very high demand right now.

Step 3: The Cold Email That Actually Gets Replies

Your email needs to be short, specific, and focused on the client — not on you. Here is the structure that works:

  1. Subject line: Mention their business name or something specific about them.
  2. Opening: One line that shows you have looked at their business — not generic flattery, something real.
  3. The problem: Name the specific problem they likely have (no website, old website, no social media presence, etc.)
  4. Your offer: One clear, low-risk offer. Not "I do web design." More like: "I build 5-page sites for dental clinics in 7 days, starting at $350."
  5. The ask: Not "hire me." Just: "Would it make sense to hop on a quick 15-minute call this week?"

Keep the whole email under 120 words. Business owners are busy. The shorter your email, the higher your reply rate.

Step 4: Following Up (This Is Where the Money Is)

Most people send one email and give up when they get no reply. This is a mistake. Research consistently shows that 60–80% of closed deals come after the 3rd to 5th follow-up. Business owners are not ignoring you — they are busy. A short, polite follow-up three days later can double your reply rate overnight.

A good follow-up sequence looks like this:

  • Day 1: Send the original email.
  • Day 4: Short follow-up: "Just checking you got my note — happy to share a quick example of what I have done for similar businesses."
  • Day 9: Value-add follow-up: "I noticed your Google Maps listing has no photos — this is the #1 thing Google uses to rank local businesses. Happy to share how to fix this quickly."
  • Day 15: Final follow-up: "I will leave you alone after this — just wanted to give you the last chance to connect. If timing is off now, feel free to reach out any time."

What About Payment? How Do You Collect Money from Abroad?

This is the question every Nigerian freelancer asks. The good news: it is easier than ever in 2026.

  • Payoneer — widely accepted, works for receiving from US clients. Withdraw to a Nigerian bank account.
  • Wise — excellent for receiving GBP and EUR from UK/European clients.
  • Grey — a Nigerian fintech that gives you a real US, UK, and EU bank account. Very popular with Nigerian freelancers right now.
  • Crypto (USDT) — many Nigerian freelancers receive in USDT and convert via Binance P2P. Fast and no intermediaries.

Quote clients in USD. A $300 website is ₦480,000+ at today's rate. One client a month covers most Nigerian household expenses. Three clients a month puts you comfortably above the average corporate salary without leaving your house.

The Honest Truth About Getting Started

None of this is complicated. But it does require you to stop waiting for opportunities to come to you and start creating them. Direct outreach is uncomfortable at first. You will send emails that get no reply. You will feel like you are wasting your time.

Push through the first 100 emails. That is when the replies start coming. That is when you land the first client. And the first client always leads to the second one — through referrals, through confidence, through having a track record you can show the next prospect.

🔑 The freelancers winning in Nigeria right now are not the most skilled — they are the most proactive. They do not wait. They pitch.
