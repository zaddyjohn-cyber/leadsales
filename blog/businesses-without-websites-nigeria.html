How to Find Businesses Without Websites to Pitch in Nigeria: A Practical Guide for Freelancers

12 July 2026 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

You're a freelancer in Nigeria—designer, copywriter, developer, marketer—and you're tired of competing on Upwork and Fiverr. Your take-home after platform fees leaves you with ₦8,000–₦15,000 on a ₦30,000 project. It's brutal.

The real money isn't in bidding wars. It's in finding businesses without websites to pitch in Nigeria. These are shops, restaurants, manufacturing firms, real estate agents, and local services that desperately need a web presence but don't know where to start. They don't hire on Upwork. They don't post jobs. But they will pay you ₦50,000–₦200,000 (USD 30–120) for a simple website, SEO audit, or brand refresh.

The catch? You have to find them first. This guide shows you exactly how.

Why Businesses Without Websites Are Your Goldmine

Let's be direct: most businesses in Nigeria aren't online. Even in 2026, according to local business surveys, only 30–40% of small and medium enterprises (SMEs) have websites. The remaining 60–70%? They're your market.

The Numbers You Need to Know

  • Average project value: ₦80,000–₦150,000 (USD 50–100) for web design, basic SEO, or branding
  • Your profit margin: 60–80% (no platform taking 20–30%)
  • Time to close a deal: 2–4 weeks from first pitch to signed contract
  • Typical client lifetime value: ₦150,000–₦400,000 over 6 months (initial work + maintenance/updates)
  • Number of pitches needed: 20–30 cold emails to land 1–2 serious leads (5–10% conversion)

If you land 2 clients per month at ₦100,000 average project fee, that's ₦200,000 monthly—roughly ₦2.4 million annually. After equipment costs and Wise/Payoneer transfer fees (3–4%), you're looking at ₦2.1 million net. That's realistic, achievable, and doesn't require a miracle.

2. Google Maps & Local Search

Open Google Maps. Search "restaurants near me" or "real estate agents Lagos" or "plumbers Ibadan." You'll see hundreds of businesses with phone numbers and addresses—many without websites (the description will say "Website: Not provided").

The method:

  1. Search your target industry + location (e.g., "fashion boutiques Lekki")
  2. Click on each business
  3. Check the "Website" field—if it's blank or says "Not provided," note their name, phone, and email (often in the description or call directly)
  4. Keep a spreadsheet with names and contact details
  5. Pitch via WhatsApp, email, or phone call

This is free and gives you 50–100 warm leads per day if you're focused. The trade-off: it's manual and slow. For volume, you need the database approach.

3. Local Business Directories & Chambers

Nigeria has hundreds of local business directories:

  • Nigerian Yellow Pages (nigerianyellopages.com)
  • Lagos Chamber of Commerce member lists
  • Nairobi Business Directory and regional equivalents
  • LinkedIn local business groups in your region
  • BudgIT's SME database (partial, but useful)

These directories list company names, industries, and contact info. Many have downloadable PDFs or lists. You won't always get email addresses, but you'll get phone numbers and you can research further.

4. WhatsApp Business Groups & Local Networks

Join WhatsApp groups for business owners in your city—"Lagos Entrepreneurs," "Abuja Business Owners," "Kano Traders Union," etc. You'll find hundreds of shop owners, salon managers, and service providers who:

  • Have no website
  • Are actively looking to grow
  • Already trust the group (warm introduction)
  • Often post about their business needs

Don't spam the group. Instead, lurk for a week, identify members by their posts, and DM them privately with a personalized pitch. This converts at 8–12% because there's already rapport.

5. Industry-Specific Events & Trade Shows

Attend Lagos Trade Fair, Abuja Investment Summit, or smaller local expos. Businesses exhibiting are growth-minded and often small enough to not have fancy websites. Collect business cards, note who has no website in their description, and follow up within 48 hours while the connection is fresh.

Cost: ₦2,000–₦5,000 entry fee. Potential: 20–40 leads per event, 5–10% conversion rate. One conversation could be a ₦100,000+ project.

6. Supply Chain Prospecting

Call manufacturers, distributors, and wholesalers. Ask, "Who are your top 10 retail customers in Lagos?" Get names, get phone numbers. Call them. Many are retailers (shops, salons, restaurants) who don't have websites. Introduce yourself as a freelancer who helps businesses like theirs build an online presence.

This is indirect but warm. The supplier vouches for you implicitly by referring you.

7. Community & Local News

Check local news sites, community boards, and online forums for newly opened businesses. New shops and restaurants are perfect prospects—they have money to spend and need immediate visibility. Follow new business registrations (some states publish these), find their owners on LinkedIn or Facebook, and pitch immediately.

How to Pitch Businesses Without Websites (The Template That Works)

Finding them is step one. Converting them is step two. Here's the formula:

Email Subject Line (High Open Rate)

Quick idea for [Business Name]'s growth or [Owner Name], your [industry] deserves online visibility

Be specific. Generic subject lines get deleted.

Email Body (4-Paragraph Rule)

  1. Observation: "I noticed [Business Name] isn't online yet, which is leaving money on the table—most customers search online before buying."
  2. Credibility: "I'm a freelancer who's helped [similar businesses in their area] build websites. Average result: 30–50% more inquiries."
  3. Offer: "I'd be happy to do a quick audit (free, no obligation) to show you what's possible."
  4. CTA: "Reply to this email or call me on [number] this week. Takes 10 minutes."

Keep it under 100 words. Busy owners skim. Once you have your list, use Zaram Web Mailer to personalize and send these at scale—it'll handle follow-ups too, so you're not manually chasing 50 emails.

Converting Leads Into Paying Clients

You get a reply. Now what?

The Discovery Call (15 Minutes)

Call them. Ask:

  • "What's your main goal right now—more customers, better branding, or both?"
  • "What's your current struggle with getting customers?"
  • "If I could solve that with a website, would that interest you?"

Listen. Don't pitch yet. Understand their pain. The goal: identify if they have budget (most do—they see the problem, they just haven't acted).

The Proposal (24–48 Hours Later)

Send a simple 1-page proposal with:

  • Problem statement: "You're losing customers because you're not online."
  • Solution: "A simple, mobile-friendly website + Google My Business optimization."
  • Deliverables: (3–5 bullets: design, copy, launch, training)
  • Timeline: "2–3 weeks from approval to live."
  • Price: ₦80,000–₦150,000 (depends on complexity)

Most will say yes within a week. Payment? Ask for 50% upfront (via bank transfer, Wise, or Payoneer). Balance on delivery. This protects you and ensures commitment.

Common Mistakes That Kill Your Conversion

  • Pitching too hard, too fast: Business owners hate being sold. Pitch the benefit (more customers), not the product (website).
  • Not doing your research: "Dear Business Owner" gets ignored. "Hi Chioma, I saw your salon on Google Maps and noticed you're not online yet"—that converts.
  • Asking for too much upfront: First-time clients are nervous. Start with a smaller project (₦50,000) to prove yourself. Upsell later.
  • No follow-up: Most replies happen on the 3rd–5th email touch. One pitch email ≠ business development. Use Zaram Web Mailer for automated, strategic follow-ups.
  • Overlooking payment logistics: Have your Wise, Payoneer, or Opay account ready. A client who wants to hire you shouldn't have to wait 2 days for you to set up transfers. That kills deals.
