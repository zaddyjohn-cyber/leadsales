12 July 2026 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

Upwork is broken for Nigerian freelancers. You're competing against 50+ people per job, bidding down your rates to ₦5,000 (about $3.40) per task, and losing 20% to platform fees. Fiverr? Worse.

There's a better way: cold email strategy for freelancers in Nigeria lets you reach businesses directly—without the middleman, without the race to the bottom, and without needing a viral portfolio.

In the next 30 minutes, I'll show you the exact system Nigerian freelancers are using to land clients paying $500–$5,000 per project. Clients who pay on time, don't haggle over rates, and come back for repeat work.

Why Cold Email Works for Nigerian Freelancers (And Why Most Fail)

Nigerian businesses are online. They need work done. But they're not hiring on Upwork—they're not even looking there. They're using Google, WhatsApp, LinkedIn, and referrals.

Cold email works because:

  • No platform fees: You keep 100% of what you earn. A ₦500,000 ($342) project stays ₦500,000. On Upwork, you'd lose ₦100,000 to fees alone.
  • Direct relationships: You're talking to the decision maker (founder, CMO, business owner), not competing with 100 freelancers.
  • Higher rates: Businesses willing to hire via cold email expect to pay premium rates. They're not price-shopping.
  • Repeat clients: Once you land one client, they hire you again and again. One client = 3–4 projects per year minimum.

But here's why most Nigerian freelancers fail at cold email: they're sending vague, generic pitches to the wrong people. They're also manually finding email addresses—a process that takes 10 hours for 100 leads.

Step 2: Craft Your Cold Email Template (Nigeria-Specific)

Your email has 3 seconds to be read. Then it's deleted or marked as spam.

Here's what doesn't work:

"Hi [Name],

I am a professional freelancer with 5 years of experience. I can help your business with various services. Please visit my portfolio and let me know if you're interested.

Best regards,
John"

That's spam. Here's what does:

Hi [Name],

I was on [Website Name], and I noticed your product pages don't show up on Google for "[Keyword]" — you're losing customers to [Competitor Name].

I helped [Similar Business] rank for 12 high-intent keywords in 60 days. They're now getting 40+ organic leads per month.

If you want a quick 15-min audit to see what's missing, let me know.

Cheers,
Tunde

The difference: specificity. You've done homework. You're not asking for their business—you're offering a small, valuable first step.

For Nigerian context, here are templates by niche:

For Web Designers:

"Hi [Name],

I noticed [Website] is using a template design—costing you credibility against competitors like [Competitor]. A lot of [Industry] businesses I work with see 25–40% higher inquiry rates after a custom redesign.

Would a quick design audit be useful?

Cheers,
[Your name]"

For Social Media Managers:

"Hi [Name],

[Competitor Name] is getting 3x more engagement on their posts than you are. I've helped 8 [Industry] brands in Lagos go from 2–3 comments per post to 20–50 in 90 days.

Happy to show you what they're doing differently.

Best,
[Your name]"

For Copywriters:

"Hi [Name],

Your product description reads like technical specs. Most visitors won't read it. I've rewritten copy for [Similar Business]—sales went up 30% in one month just from the homepage change.

Want to see an example?

[Your name]"

Notice the pattern:

  1. Observation: Something specific about their business
  2. Social proof: A number-based result you've achieved (or a competitor has)
  3. Small ask: A call to a 15-min audit, not a sales pitch
  4. Signature: Just your name, nothing else

Subject line matters. Use these:

  • "Quick question about [their product/service]"
  • "I found 3 things losing you customers"
  • "[Competitor] is outranking you—here's why"
  • "Audit: your [service] page"

Avoid: "We can help!", "Let's work together", "Free consultation" (everyone sends these).

Step 4: Follow-Up Like a Professional (Not Desperate)

80% of deals happen in the follow-up. The first email gets ignored. The second one might too. The third one gets a reply.

Here's your follow-up sequence:

Day 1: Initial email (as written above)

Day 4: Follow-up (if no reply)
"Hi [Name],

Didn't hear back—probably too busy 😊

I mentioned [specific problem]. Just wanted to drop this in case it's worth a quick look.

All the best,
[Your name]"

Day 9: Second follow-up
"Hi [Name],

Last one—promise 😊

I've helped 6 other [Industry] businesses solve this. If you're interested in a free 15-min chat, I'm free [day/time].

No pressure if it's not a fit.

[Your name]"

After that: Stop. Move to the next list.

Key rule: never be needy. You're offering value. They either want it or they don't.

Step 5: Qualify and Close (Real Conversations, Real Money)

If they reply, your job shifts from "get attention" to "qualify the lead" and "set expectations."

Before jumping on a call, ask qualifying questions via email:

  • "What's your main blocker with [service I offer]?"
  • "What budget are you working with for this project?"
  • "When are you looking to start?"

Why? Because a 30-minute call with someone who has no budget is a waste. If they won't answer budget questions in email, they won't pay you fairly in a project.

Once you qualify them:

  1. Jump on a quick call (15–20 mins). Use Google Meet or Zoom.
  2. Ask about their pain point, timeline, and budget.
  3. Send a proposal (not a quote—a detailed scope of work with price) within 24 hours.
  4. If they approve, onboard them, get a 50% deposit (via Wise, Payoneer, or their Nigerian bank), and start work.

Real numbers: a qualified lead from cold email converts to a paying client 40–60% of the time (after a call). Compare that to Upwork (2–5% conversion rate on proposals) or Fiverr (even lower).

Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Mistake 1: Generic lead lists
Downloading a list of "all businesses in Nigeria" is useless. You'll email restaurants, barbers, and plumbers who don't need your services. Use Freelance LeadsHub and filter by niche (web design, digital marketing, etc.) and city (Lagos, Abuja, etc.). Precision > volume.

Mistake 2: Trying to close on email
"Interested? Here's my Fiverr link" doesn't work. People buy from people, not links. Get them on a call. Sell on the call. Send proposal after.

Mistake 3: No follow-ups
Sending one email and waiting is lazy. Real cold email is a 3-email sequence over 10 days. Most replies come on emails 2–3.

Mistake 4: Sending from Gmail without a tool
You'll hit spam filters, get rate-limited, and manually track replies (chaos). Use Zaram Web Mailer or a similar tool to handle sending, follow-ups, and tracking automatically.

Mistake 5: Poor subject lines and openers
"Hi [Name]" is how every generic email starts. Use a question or observation: "Question about your [product]" or "[Your competitor] is beating you on Google." This gets opens.
