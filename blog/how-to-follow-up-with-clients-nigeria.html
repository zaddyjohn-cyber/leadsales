How to Follow Up With Potential Clients as a Freelancer in Nigeria

In this guide, I'm giving you the exact follow-up framework I've seen work for freelancers landing ₦500,000+ contracts from cold outreach. No theory. Just what works.

Why Most Nigerian Freelancers Fail at Follow-Up

Let me be blunt: you're probably not following up at all, or you're doing it wrong.

Here's what typically happens:

  • You send one email and wait for a response like you're waiting for NEPA to fix the power
  • After one week, you assume they're not interested
  • You move on to the next prospect without ever coming back
  • Meanwhile, that client hired someone else—or never even saw your first email

According to data from cold email campaigns globally, the average response rate to a single email is 1-5%. That means 95-99 out of 100 people won't respond to your first touch. In Nigeria, where inboxes are flooded and business owners are overwhelmed, that number is likely even lower.

But here's the good news: follow-up works. Studies show that the 5th to 7th touch point is when most conversions happen. Most freelancers never get there. You will.

Reality Check: A prospect ignoring your email doesn't mean they're not interested. It usually means they're busy, your email landed in spam, or they forgot about it. The freelancers who actually make money are the ones who follow up respectfully and strategically.

The Right Timeline for Following Up in Nigeria

Timing matters. Too fast and you seem desperate. Too slow and they forget you exist.

Here's the follow-up sequence that actually works:

  1. Initial Email (Day 0): Your first pitch. Keep it short—under 100 words. Link to your portfolio. Make it about their problem, not about you.
  2. First Follow-Up (Day 3-4): Light touch. Reference your previous email. Add one new piece of value—maybe a relevant article, case study, or quick insight about their business. Don't demand a response.
  3. Second Follow-Up (Day 10-12): A different angle. Maybe they weren't convinced by your first angle. Try a new one. Different subject line. Different approach.
  4. Third Follow-Up (Day 21): Patience here. You're now 3 weeks out. This email should be short and human. "Wanted to check if you're still working on [specific project]. Happy to help when you're ready."
  5. Fourth Follow-Up (Day 35): Final push. Make it count. Include a specific case study or result. If they still don't respond, archive them for now.

Why this timeline? Nigerian business owners are often juggling multiple vendors, dealing with currency fluctuations, and managing inconsistent internet. A 3-week cycle gives them multiple "moments of awareness" without being annoying.

Crafting Follow-Up Emails That Actually Convert

Your follow-up can't be a carbon copy of the first email. It needs to be different—different subject line, different angle, different value prop.

Follow-Up Email 1 (Day 3-4): The Value Add

Subject Line: "Quick thought on [their business/project]"

Structure:

  • Reference your previous email (one line only)
  • Share one specific insight or resource relevant to them
  • Soft call-to-action: "Let me know if this helps"
  • Keep it under 50 words

Example:

"Hi [Name], Following up on my previous email. I noticed you're in [industry]. Most businesses in your space are losing 30-40% of leads due to slow website response times. Happy to run a quick audit if you want. Let me know."

Follow-Up Email 2 (Day 10-12): The Case Study

Subject Line: "Similar client, ₦2.5M result"

Structure:

  • Short story about a similar business you've worked with
  • The specific problem they had
  • The result (in concrete terms: time saved, revenue gained, or conversions increased)
  • One line: "Thought you should see this"

Example:

"Hi [Name], A construction company similar to yours was losing ₦50,000+ monthly to poor project tracking. We built them a custom CRM. 90 days later, they recovered ₦2.5M in lost bids. Could work for you. Thoughts?"

Follow-Up Email 3 (Day 21): The Check-In

Subject Line: "Just checking in"

Structure:

  • Short and human. No pitch.
  • Acknowledge they might be busy
  • Leave the door open

Example:

"Hi [Name], I know you're busy. Just wanted to check if you're still looking at this or if it's not a priority right now. Either way, no pressure. My door's open if you need me down the road."

Common Follow-Up Mistakes Nigerian Freelancers Make

Mistake 1: Being Too Aggressive

Sending 5 follow-ups in 10 days will get you marked as spam. Stick to the timeline. Patience converts.

Mistake 2: Sending the Same Email Over and Over

If your first email didn't work, the second one shouldn't be identical. Change the subject line. Change the angle. Change the value prop.

Mistake 3: Not Personalizing

Generic emails get deleted. Use their name. Reference their business by name. Show you know something about them. In Nigeria, business owners can smell mass emails from a mile away.

Mistake 4: Following Up on Bad Leads

Not every prospect is worth 4-5 follow-ups. If someone's not in your ideal client profile, abandon them sooner. That's why getting a clean, targeted list from how to get clients as a freelancer in Nigeria beats random scraping.

Mistake 5: Forgetting the Phone

Email is great, but it's not the whole story. If you have their phone number, a quick WhatsApp or call after your Day 10 email can be gold. "Hi [Name], sent you an email about [topic]. Quick question: Is this something you'd consider?" A 30-second call beats 5 emails.
