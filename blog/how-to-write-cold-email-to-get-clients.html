Why Cold Email Works Better Than Upwork for Nigerian Freelancers

Let me be direct: cold email is not about sending 1,000 emails and hoping. It's about sending 50–100 highly targeted emails to businesses that actually need what you offer—and converting 5–10% of them into paying clients.

Here's why it works:

  • No platform commission. You keep 100% of what you earn. A ₦500,000 project stays ₦500,000—not ₦400,000 after Upwork's cut.
  • Better quality clients. You're reaching business owners and decision-makers directly, not competing with 200 other bidders.
  • Higher project rates. Direct clients typically pay 40–60% more than marketplace rates because there's no "race to the bottom."
  • Repeat business. Once you land a client, you build a relationship. They call you again. That's predictable income.

Real numbers: A Nigerian web designer earning ₦80,000–120,000 per project on Upwork can charge ₦300,000–500,000 for the same project through cold email to small businesses. At current exchange rates (~₦1,600 to $1 USD), that's the difference between $50–75 and $180–300 per project.

The Cold Email Formula That Actually Works

Before you write a single email, understand this: Nigerian businesses are skeptical of unsolicited emails. They've been burnt by scammers, overpromised vendors, and get hundreds of emails weekly. Your job is to stand out by being specific, brief, and genuinely helpful.

Here's the structure:

1. Subject Line: Make Them Open It

Your subject line determines if the email gets read or deleted. Here's what works:

  • Personalized and curious: "Quick question about [Business Name]'s website"
  • Specific benefit: "How [Competitor] increased their leads by 35% this month"
  • Local relevance: "Lagos-based web designer here"
  • Question-based: "Are you handling [specific pain point] in-house?"

Bad subject lines to avoid:

  • "Let's work together!" (sounds like spam)
  • "URGENT: Your website needs help" (spammy and aggressive)
  • "Hi there" (not personalized)

What works best for Nigeria: Use a subject line that shows you've done research on their business. Example: "Saw your new Lagos showroom—your website could drive more foot traffic."

2. The Opening: Build Credibility in Two Sentences

Skip the fluff. Don't say "I hope this email finds you well." Nobody cares. Start with:

  • A genuine compliment about their business (not their "amazing company culture"—be specific)
  • Proof that you've actually looked at their website, product, or recent news

Example:

"Hi Chioma, I noticed you just launched your new FinTech product on Product Hunt last week. I've built similar financial dashboards for three other startups in Lagos."

This tells them: You're not mass-mailing. You know who they are. You've done relevant work.

3. The Problem: Make It About Them, Not You

This is where most Nigerian freelancers fail. They write:

"Hi, I'm a web designer with 5 years of experience in web design and graphic design. I can help you with your website..."

Nobody cares about your experience yet. They care about their problem.

Instead, identify a specific pain point their business likely has:

  • If they're an e-commerce brand: "Your product pages aren't converting visitors into buyers"
  • If they're a service business: "You're losing clients because your booking system is manual"
  • If they're a B2B company: "Your website isn't generating qualified leads"

Example:

"Most beauty brands in Lagos are stuck with Jumia or Instagram for sales. They're paying 15–20% commission and have zero control over their customer data."

4. The Solution: Your Service (Keep It Brief)

Now mention what you do, but frame it as a solution to their problem. One sentence. Maximum.

Bad: "I specialize in web design, UI/UX, branding, and digital marketing solutions."

Good: "I build custom e-commerce stores that let beauty brands sell directly and keep 100% of revenue."

The good version is specific, shows the benefit, and speaks to their business model.

5. Proof: Show You've Done This Before

You don't need a portfolio for every email, but you need proof. This can be:

  • A quick case study: "I built a store for [Brand Name] that went from ₦200K/month to ₦2M/month in six months."
  • A client testimonial: "One of my clients said: 'This reduced our design turnaround from 4 weeks to 2 weeks.'"
  • A metric: "I've trained 12 small business owners in Lagos on content marketing."

Keep it real. Nigerian businesses appreciate honesty. If you don't have case studies yet, mention the types of businesses you've worked with or a specific skill result.

6. The CTA: Make One Clear Ask

Don't ask for a meeting. That's intimidating. Ask for a small conversation or information:

  • "Do you have 15 minutes next week for a quick call?"
  • "Would it make sense to see a quick mockup of what this could look like for your site?"
  • "Are you open to a conversation about this?"

Best CTA for Nigeria: "Can I send you a quick two-minute video showing how this worked for a similar brand?" People respond to video and visual proofs.

7. Sign-Off: Keep It Professional and Local

Skip "Best regards" and "Warm wishes." Use:

  • "Cheers,"
  • "Talk soon,"
  • "Looking forward to hearing from you,"

Include your name, phone number (WhatsApp preferred in Nigeria), and a link to your best work sample or website.

Real Example: Here's a cold email that converted for a Nigerian UI designer:

"Hi Tunde, I noticed your fintech app just hit 50K users on TechCrunch. Most apps at that stage hit a wall with user retention—usually because the onboarding flow is too complex. I redesigned the signup flow for Paystack's competitor last year, reducing drop-off from 35% to 12%. Would it make sense to jump on a quick call to see if we could do the same for you? Cheers, Amara | +234-XXX-XXXX"

This email has specificity (50K users, TechCrunch), proof (Paystack-level work), and a clear, small ask. It converted into a ₦800,000 project.

Common Cold Email Mistakes Nigerian Freelancers Make

Mistake 1: Being Too Formal or Desperate

Don't write like a bank letter. Nigerian business owners are people. They appreciate friendly, direct communication. "Looking forward to an opportunity to collaborate" sounds desperate. "Let me know if you'd like to chat" sounds confident.

Mistake 2: Not Personalizing At All

Mass emails to 500 identical recipients will get low response rates. Spend 30 seconds personalizing each email—mention something specific about their business, their recent news, or their website.

Mistake 3: Making It About You

Don't lead with your experience, certifications, or how long you've been freelancing. Lead with what's in it for them. "I help e-commerce brands in Lagos increase sales by 40%" beats "I'm a certified web designer with 6 years of experience."

Mistake 4: No Clear CTA

Don't send an email and hope. Ask for something specific: a call, a reply, 15 minutes of their time. The ask should be small and easy to say yes to.

Mistake 5: Not Following Up

Most replies come on the second or third email. Send an initial email, wait 5–7 days, send a follow-up. Wait another week, send one more. That's it—don't become annoying. Many Nigerian freelancers quit after one email.
