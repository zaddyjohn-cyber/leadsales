How to Get Website Design Clients from Abroad in Nigeria: A Practical Guide to Cold Outreach & International Sales

July 23, 2026 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

If you're a Nigerian web designer, you already know the frustration. Upwork is crowded. Fiverr takes 20% commission. Platforms are race-to-the-bottom pricing wars where you're competing against designers in Bangladesh willing to work for ₦3,000 per project.

But here's what most Nigerian freelancers don't realize: small businesses abroad—especially in the UK, US, Canada, and Australia—are desperate for quality web design. They'll pay $2,000–$10,000 (₦3.2M–₦16M) for a professional website. And they don't know you exist because you're not in their network.

The solution? Stop waiting for clients to find you. Go find them. This guide walks you through exactly how to get website design clients from abroad in Nigeria using cold email and strategic outreach.

Why Nigerian Web Designers Should Target International Clients

Let's be honest about the numbers first.

  • Local Nigerian market: A business website in Lagos might fetch ₦150,000–₦500,000. Timeline: 2–3 weeks. You're competing with 50+ other designers.
  • International clients: That same website from a UK accountant or Australian coach? $3,000–$8,000 (₦4.8M–₦12.8M at today's rates). Timeline: same 2–3 weeks. Less competition, higher budgets.

One international client per month = ₦4.8M–₦12.8M annually. That's ₦57.6M–₦153.6M per year from just a handful of clients. A senior web designer in a Nigerian agency earns ₦60,000–₦150,000 monthly. Do the math.

The second reason: currency arbitrage. A $5,000 project is painful for a UK small business owner. For you in Nigeria, it's transformational income. You can cover your data costs (Glo unlimited bundles: ₦5,000–₦8,000/month), rent, and still have a comfortable buffer—especially with services like Wise or Payoneer that give you better exchange rates than Nigerian banks.

Manually researching this is brutal. You'd spend 20 hours finding 50 prospects. Even then, half won't be the right fit.

Step 1: Define Your Niche (Don't Chase Everyone)

Before you download leads, know who you're looking for.

A common mistake: "I do websites for anyone." That's a weak pitch. Instead:

  • Accountants/bookkeepers (they're understaffed, need websites urgently, have budgets)
  • E-commerce stores (selling physical products, need traffic and conversions)
  • Coaches/consultants (solopreneurs, high margins, will pay for professional branding)
  • Real estate agents/agencies (need portfolio sites, property listings, lead capture)
  • Service-based businesses (plumbers, electricians, therapists—surprisingly good clients)

Pick one or two. It's easier to become the "web designer for UK accountants" than the "web designer for everyone." You'll sound credible. You'll know their pain points. Your portfolio will speak directly to their needs.

Why This Matters for Your Outreach

When you email a UK accountant with a subject line like "Website redesign for accountancy practices" instead of "Web design services," your open rate doubles. They think you understand them (because you do).

Step 3: Write Your Cold Email Script

Your email needs to do three things:

  1. Grab attention (reference something specific about their business)
  2. State the problem they have (outdated website, no mobile optimization, low conversions)
  3. Offer a quick next step (not "let's chat"—be specific)

Template That Works for Nigerian Designers

Subject: Quick thought on [Company Name]'s website

Hi [First Name],

I noticed your site is built on an older platform and doesn't have a mobile booking system—that's costing you leads from clients trying to book on their phones.

I've helped [X] UK [accountants/coaches/stores] redesign their sites for conversions. Most see a 40–60% boost in inquiry submissions within 2 months of launch.

Would a 20-min call make sense to discuss what's possible? I can share a quick audit of your current site.

Best,
[Your Name]

Why this works:

  • It's specific (you reference their actual site issue)
  • It shows proof (you've done this for others like them)
  • It's not salesy (no "special offer," no pressure)
  • It asks for a small commitment (20 minutes, not a sales call)

Step 5: Follow Up (Most Don't—That's Your Edge)

If you send one email and don't hear back, you assume they're not interested. Wrong.

Industry data: 5–8 touches (emails, LinkedIn messages, light phone calls) are needed before someone responds. Most Nigerian freelancers send one email and move on. That's why you'll outcompete them.

Your follow-up sequence (using Zaram Web Mailer automation):

  1. Email 1 (Day 1): Initial pitch
  2. Email 2 (Day 4): "Didn't catch you—here's a quick case study of similar work"
  3. Email 3 (Day 10): "One more thing—happy to audit your site for free if you've got 10 minutes"
  4. LinkedIn outreach (Week 2): Add them on LinkedIn with a personalized message
  5. Light phone call (Week 3, if they're in a reachable timezone): "Saw you're in the accounting space—wanted to check if you're exploring a rebrand soon"

This consistency converts. Not everyone responds to email. Some prefer calls. Some need to see your name twice before they trust you. Persist.

Step 6: Build Social Proof & Portfolio

When they Google your name or check your website, what do they see?

Your portfolio matters. Before you start cold outreach, ensure you have:

  • 3–5 strong case studies (international work, if possible)
  • Client testimonials with names and photos
  • A clean personal website showcasing your best work
  • LinkedIn with a professional photo and endorsements
  • A simple one-pager PDF you can send showing past projects

This doesn't need to be fancy. One-page PDFs with before/after screenshots, client name, and a short quote ("This designer understood our market. Website conversions up 45%.") suffice. Place them on Google Drive and share the link in your follow-up emails.

Common Mistakes Nigerian Freelancers Make

Avoid these:

  • Pitching too many people, too generally: "I do web design" lands in spam. "I redesign e-commerce sites for 6–7 figure stores" converts.
  • Not following up: One email gets you nowhere. Send 5. Most close on email 3–5.
  • Copying your portfolio from Upwork gigs: International clients want to see international work or strong results. Update your case studies to show real business impact (conversions, leads generated).
  • Giving weak timelines: Say "We can start next Monday, delivery in 4 weeks" not "Let me check my schedule." Sound professional and decisive.
  • Not using a CRM or email tracking: You send 200 emails and forget who you emailed. Use Zaram Web Mailer to track opens and automate follow-ups. Your brain isn't a database.

What About Payment & Delivery?

Payment methods: Use Wise, Payoneer, or Stripe to receive payments. USD arrives in your Nigerian bank account at real-time rates (you avoid the ₦50/$ middleman premium your bank charges).

Milestone-based payment: Ask for 50% upfront (design phase), 50% on delivery. Protects both of you.

Contracts: Use a simple Upwork-style agreement or Fiverr's format as a template. Protects you from scope creep ("Just add one more feature..."). Makes it professional.

Delivery timeline: 4–6 weeks is standard. Give yourself breathing room. Nigerian internet isn't always reliable; NEPA outages happen. Build a buffer.

Pro tip: International clients respect Nigerian designers who are upfront about timezones and communication. "I'm in Lagos, online 9 AM–6 PM WAT (GMT+1). Responses within 24 hours guaranteed" builds trust.

Scaling Beyond Cold Email

Once you've nailed cold outreach and closed a few clients, add these channels:

  • Referrals: Ask past clients for introductions. "If you know any other [accountants/coaches] who need a site, I'd love to chat with them."
  • LinkedIn content: Post 2–3x weekly about web design for your niche. "5 mistakes accounting practices make on their websites" gets traction and attracts inbound leads.
  • Partnerships: Team up with UK/US design agencies who outsource development. You become their trusted Nigerian partner for project overflow.
  • Community groups: Find Facebook groups, Slack communities, or forums where your niche hangs out. Join, be helpful, mention what you do naturally.

Cold email remains the most scalable channel. But a mix reduces your risk.
