How to Get Video Editing Clients in Nigeria: A Direct Outreach Strategy

2026-07-24 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

If you're a video editor in Nigeria competing on Upwork or Fiverr, you already know the reality: endless bidding wars, platform fees eating 20% of your earnings, and clients who want professional-grade edits for ₦5,000.

Here's what actually works: Go direct to businesses that need video editing but aren't looking on freelance platforms.

This guide shows you exactly how to get video editing clients in Nigeria by reaching out to businesses with real budgets—SMEs, agencies, content creators, and ecommerce stores—and why this approach will earn you 3–5x more than competing with thousands of editors on marketplace sites.

Why Most Nigerian Video Editors Stay Broke

The problem isn't your skill. The problem is where you're looking for work.

On Upwork, a basic video editing job (product video, testimonial, social media cut) pays $30–80 USD (roughly ₦20,000–₦54,000 at current rates). After Upwork's 20% cut and payment processing fees, you're looking at ₦16,000–₦43,000 for 8–15 hours of work. That's ₦1,000–₦5,000 per hour—barely above minimum wage.

But businesses that don't post on Upwork? They have bigger budgets.

  • Marketing agencies need monthly video editing (ads, case studies, reels) and pay ₦200K–₦1M per project
  • ecommerce stores produce product videos for TikTok and Instagram—recurring monthly work at ₦150K–₦400K
  • Real estate and finance companies need explainer videos and testimonials—usually ₦250K–₦750K projects
  • Content creators with 10K+ followers pay ₦100K–₦300K monthly for editing and thumbnails

These businesses exist in Nigeria right now. You just need to find them and reach out directly.

Step 3: Write a Pitch That Actually Works

Your email needs to solve a specific pain point. Don't say "I do video editing." Say:

"Hi [Name],

I noticed [Company] publishes product videos on Instagram. Most SMEs lose 40% of potential sales because their videos don't hook viewers in the first 3 seconds.

I work with ecommerce stores to create fast-paced, high-converting product videos (30–90 sec). Typical project: ₦150K–₦400K. Turnaround: 5–7 days.

If you're looking for a reliable video editor who understands mobile-first content, let's chat.

[Your name]"

Notice what this does:

  • Shows you researched them (specific)
  • Identifies a clear problem (poor video hooks)
  • Offers a solution (fast editing, mobile-first)
  • Sets clear expectations (price, timeline)
  • Has a low-friction CTA ("let's chat")

Key insight: Personalization doesn't mean writing unique emails for 500 people. It means using their company name, referencing something specific about their business, and naming your exact offer. You can send 50 personalized emails per hour using templates in Zaram Web Mailer.

Step 4: Follow Up (Most Emails Are Ignored On First Contact)

Email open rates in Nigeria are 15–25%. Response rates are 2–5%. That means 95% of your first emails will go unanswered.

This is normal and expected. It's not personal.

Send a follow-up 5 days later if there's no reply:

"Hi [Name],

Happy to share examples of product videos I've edited for [relevant industry]. No pressure either way.

[Your name]"

Common Objections—Solved

"Won't They Think I'm Spam?"

No. You're not sending unsolicited ads. You're reaching out to businesses that need your service but don't know you exist. It's how B2B works in every industry. Agencies cold email startups. Accounting firms cold email SMEs. This is legitimate business development.

"I Don't Have a Fancy Portfolio Yet"

You don't need one. Show 3–5 best pieces, even if they're from personal projects or friends' businesses. Quality > quantity. One impressive 90-second product video beats 20 mediocre clips.

"What If My Internet Cuts Out During a Project?"

That's real in Nigeria. Build it into your timeline. Tell clients you deliver in 5–7 days, not 3. Save work locally and to cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox) every 30 minutes. NEPA shutdowns are predictable—schedule rendering during daylight hours.

"I Need Cash Now, Not in 3 Months"

Fair. Do both: keep doing Upwork for immediate income while you build a cold email pipeline. Spend 5 hours/week on outreach, 20 hours/week on Upwork work. As cold email clients come through, shift the ratio.

Payment Logistics for Nigerian Video Editors

Getting paid is the last piece. Here's how it works:

For local clients (₦ transfers): Use Paystack, Flutterwave, or direct bank transfer. Charge a 5% processing fee to cover their cut. Most Nigerian SMEs understand this.

For international/USD clients: Use Payoneer, Wise, or Grey. Wise is fastest for NGN conversion (0.5–1% fee). Payoneer takes longer but is reliable. Grey is newer but has good rates.

Retainer payments: Request 50% upfront, 50% on delivery. Or split monthly: ₦100K on the 1st, rest by the 15th. Set this before work starts.

Invoicing: Use Wave (free) or Zoho Invoice. Send invoices before delivery, not after. This signals professionalism.
