How to Get International Clients in Nigeria: A Cold Email & Lead Strategy for 2026

17 July 2026 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

If you're a freelancer in Nigeria, you already know the reality: competing on Upwork and Fiverr is brutal. Rates are slashed, competition is global, and your take-home after platform fees and exchange-rate losses is nowhere near what you need. The median Upwork rate for Nigerian writers is ₦2,500–₦4,000 per 500 words. On Fiverr, you're looking at even less.

But here's what most Nigerian freelancers don't realise: international clients exist who will pay 10–20x more — and they're not looking for you on Upwork. They're running their own websites, managing their own email inboxes, and they need help. The question isn't "where are the clients?" — it's "how do I reach them directly?"

This guide shows you exactly how to get international clients in Nigeria by skipping the platform grind and going directly to businesses that can afford your rates.

Why International Clients Pay Better (And Why Nigerians Miss Out)

Let's start with numbers that matter to you.

A Nigerian freelancer charging ₦50,000 per article on Upwork actually takes home around ₦35,000–₦40,000 after fees and exchange-rate loss (if converted via Payoneer). At the current naira-to-dollar rate (roughly ₦1,540–₦1,600 per USD), that's about $22–$26 USD.

A US-based SaaS company will pay $150–$500 for the same article. That's not hype — that's the real gap between platform rates and direct-client rates.

Why does this happen?

  • Platforms commoditise your work. You're competing against 50,000 other writers. Upwork's algorithm favours low prices and fast delivery, not quality.
  • Currency arbitrage is built into platforms. Upwork, Fiverr, and similar sites know you'll accept low rates because even ₦20,000 is money to you. They pocket the difference.
  • International businesses don't use these platforms. Fortune 500 companies, mid-market SaaS firms, and serious e-commerce stores hire through direct outreach, referrals, or agencies — not freelance marketplaces.
  • Direct clients pay per value delivered, not per hour or task. They're paying for results, not your time zone or platform membership.

The Real Income Gap: A Nigerian freelancer charging ₦50,000 ($32 USD) on Upwork can charge $250–$400 to a direct US client for identical work. That's 8–12x more. Over a year, the difference between ₦2.4M (platform work) and ₦19–30M (direct clients at just 3–4 clients per month) is the difference between surviving and thriving.

Step 1: Choose Your Niche & Identify Who Needs You

Before you reach out to anyone, you need a clear answer to this question: Who specifically will pay me the most?

Don't say "I'm a writer" or "I do web design." That doesn't work. Instead, choose a niche based on two criteria:

  1. Willingness to pay: Which industries and business types pay the highest rates?
  2. Pain point match: What problem do you solve that they feel acutely?

Here are high-paying niches that international clients actively hire for (with realistic rates):

  • SaaS & Tech Content: Companies writing landing pages, blog posts, and case studies. Rate: $200–$500 per article. Why? They're selling software subscriptions worth thousands per year.
  • E-commerce SEO: Shopify, WooCommerce stores optimising for search. Rate: $1,500–$5,000 per project. Why? A single customer is worth $100+; they'll invest in getting found.
  • Paid Ads Management: Facebook, Google Ads, TikTok Ads for digital products. Rate: $1,500–$3,500 per month retainer. Why? A 20% ROI on ad spend is a win; they'll pay for management.
  • Email Marketing & Copywriting: Funnel copy, VSL scripts, sequences. Rate: $500–$2,000 per project. Why? A single email campaign can generate $50,000+ in revenue; copywriting is a direct profit centre.
  • Web Design (Custom & WordPress): Branding agencies, coaches, local service businesses. Rate: $1,500–$5,000 per site. Why? A website generates consistent leads and sales.
  • Video Editing (Short-form): YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Reels for coaches and creators. Rate: $500–$1,500 per 10 videos. Why? Video content is the fastest-growing format; supply is low.

Pick one. Narrow it down further. For example, instead of "SaaS Content," say "SEO blog posts for B2B SaaS companies in the sales software space." Instead of "Web Design," say "Shopify store design for sustainable fashion brands."

This matters because when you reach out, you'll mention specific problems they face. Vague pitches get deleted. Specific, targeted pitches get responses.

Step 2: Build a Targeted Lead List (The Unglamorous But Critical Part)

Now you know who you're looking for. Next step: find them.

This is where most Nigerian freelancers fail. They hope to stumble into clients through networking or luck. That doesn't work at scale. You need a list of actual business email addresses and decision-makers you can pitch.

Here's what you need for each prospect:

  • Business name
  • Decision-maker (founder, CEO, marketing manager)
  • Email address
  • Phone number (optional but helpful)
  • Website URL

How to build this list:

Option A: Manual research (slow, free, doesn't scale)

  • Google "[niche] companies" + "United States" or specific country
  • Visit their websites, find Contact pages or LinkedIn profiles
  • Manually copy emails into a spreadsheet
  • Time investment: 5–10 hours to build a list of 50 prospects. Exhausting and error-prone.

Option B: Paid databases (fast, accurate, scalable)

Tools like Hunter.io, RocketReach, Apollo.io, and ZoomInfo can find emails by company or industry. But they're expensive ($300–$2,000+ per month) and overkill if you're just starting out.

Option C: Use a lead list service built for freelancers

This is where Freelance LeadsHub comes in. You download pre-researched, categorised lead lists (verified names, emails, phone numbers, websites) by niche and country. For example, you could download "SaaS founders in the US interested in content marketing" or "Shopify store owners in the UK." The lists are updated regularly and cost a fraction of annual subscriptions to traditional B2B databases.

Why this works: You're not spending 40 hours researching; you're spending 2 hours filtering and personalising outreach. That's the freelancer advantage — low overhead, high output.

Start with a list of 50–100 prospects. Don't aim for 1,000 yet. You want to test your message first.

Step 3: Write a Cold Email That Actually Works

A cold email is your opening pitch. It's not an essay, not a sales page, and definitely not a copy-paste template. It's a short, personalised message that shows you understand the prospect's business and have a specific solution.

The anatomy of a cold email that gets replies:

  1. Subject line (the gatekeeper): "Quick question about [Company Name]'s blog" or "Your Shopify store + [specific observation]"
  2. Opening (proof you did research): "I noticed you recently launched X" or "I saw your TikTok video got 100k views but your website isn't optimised for conversions."
  3. The hook (why you, why now): "I help [similar companies] increase [specific metric] by doing [your service]. [Example/proof]."
  4. Soft close (easy next step): "Open to a 15-minute call this week?"

Example for a SaaS copywriter:

Hi [First Name],

I was looking at your landing page for [Product]. Your value prop is clear, but I noticed your CTA conversion rate (assuming) is probably in the 2–3% range — typical for SaaS pages without specific, benefit-driven copy.

I've rewritten copy for 8 B2B SaaS companies this year. On average, their conversion lifted 40–60% after I rewrote their homepage and email sequences.

Worth a quick call to see if it applies to your business?

Best,
[Your Name]

Notice: no fluff, one clear benefit, one proof point, one ask.

Step 5: Close the Deal (It's Easier Than You Think)

Once someone replies to your cold email, you've already won half the battle. They're interested. Your job now is to:

  1. Schedule a call (not via email back-and-forth). Say: "Let's jump on a quick call Thursday at 2 PM ET. Here's my Calendly link."
  2. On the call, ask questions first. Don't pitch. Understand their problem deeply.
  3. Show you can solve it. "Based on what you've told me, here's what I'd do..." (be specific)
  4. Quote a fixed price based on deliverables, not hours. Not "I charge $50/hour" but "$3,000 to rewrite your 10 landing pages and A/B test them over 4 weeks."
  5. Use a contract. Even a simple one-page agreement protects you and them. (See tools like Notion or Stripe Contracts.)

On payment: International clients will use Wise (formerly TransferWise), Stripe, or PayPal. Set up a Wise account (it's free and gives you a US/UK/EU bank account). This cuts your transaction fees from 3–5% to 0.5–1.5% and removes the naira FX loss.

A $3,000 project via Wise lands in your Wise USD account as $3,000. You convert to NGN at the real rate (no platform markup) and withdraw to your Nigerian bank. Much better than Payoneer.

Payment Setup Matters: A $5,000 project costs you ₦800 in Wise fees vs. ₦25,000 in Payoneer + Upwork fees. Over 10 projects a year, that's ₦240,000 saved — just by using the right payment tool.

Common Mistakes Nigerian Freelancers Make (And How to Avoid Them)

  • Not specific enough with their niche. "I'm a designer" doesn't work. "I design high-converting Shopify stores for sustainable fashion brands" does.
  • Underselling because of currency. If a US client would pay $5,000, don't charge $2,000 because "that's good money in Nigeria." Charge the market rate. Your value doesn't change based on where you live.
  • Trying to reach too many people at once. 50 personalised cold emails get 2–5 replies. 500 spammy emails get 0. Start small, test, refine.
  • Using Upwork/Fiverr as your main channel while building direct clients. You can't do both well. Pick direct outreach as your growth channel; treat platforms as a side income only.
  • Not following up. Most replies come after the 2nd or 3rd email. Don't send once and give up.
  • Accepting projects outside their niche to pay bills. This kills your positioning. One month of food money isn't worth six months of confused messaging.
