How to Find Web Design Clients in Nigeria Without Upwork

24 July 2026 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

If you're a web designer in Nigeria, you already know the Upwork trap. You build a perfect portfolio, set your rates at ₦50,000–₦150,000 per project, and then watch clients haggle you down to ₦15,000 because there's always someone willing to work cheaper. Your notifications blow up, but nobody bites at fair rates. The platform takes 20% commission. Clients disappear mid-project. The algorithm pushes newer freelancers ahead of you.

There's a better way. Instead of competing with thousands of freelancers for scraps, you can find web design clients in Nigeria directly—businesses that actually need what you build and will pay real money for it. We're talking ₦200,000–₦1,000,000+ per project, not per hour.

This guide shows you exactly how to do it. No platform drama. No bidding wars. Just direct access to businesses that hire web designers.

Why Upwork Doesn't Work for Nigerian Web Designers

Let's be real about the numbers:

  • Commission theft: Upwork takes 20% of your first $500 earned with a client, then 10% after. On a ₦500,000 project (roughly $300 USD), you lose ₦100,000 to platform fees.
  • Race to the bottom: The average Upwork bid for a "simple website" in Nigeria sits around ₦10,000–₦30,000. You can't survive on that after paying for hosting, domain renewal, and data costs.
  • Dead time between projects: Most Nigerian web designers spend 60–70% of their time on Upwork hunting for the next gig instead of doing actual design work.
  • Client quality: Many Upwork clients are one-time buyers or budget-conscious businesses that disappear after project completion. No repeat work. No referrals. No growth.

A typical Nigerian web designer on Upwork makes ₦200,000–₦400,000 per month after fees and failed bids. That's roughly $120–$240 USD. Liveable, but fragile. One bad month means tight finances.

Direct outreach changes everything. When you land a client directly—say, a retail business or an e-commerce startup that needs a site—you keep 100% of your fees. A single ₦500,000 project becomes ₦500,000 in your account, not ₦400,000 after commissions. Scale to 2–3 projects per month, and you're earning ₦1–₦1.5 million monthly. That's sustainable, respected income.

2. LinkedIn Outreach (Organic, Free, Slow)

LinkedIn is where Nigerian business owners actually hang out. They scroll between meetings, see posts from other entrepreneurs, and think about business growth.

Build a lean LinkedIn strategy:

  • Niche down your headline: Don't say "Web Designer." Say "Web Designer for E-commerce & Retail Businesses in Nigeria—I Help You Sell Online." This attracts the right audience.
  • Post once weekly: Share before/after website designs, quick wins from past projects, or articles about web design ROI. Nigerian business owners care about *results*, not design trends.
  • DM cold outreach: Send 20–30 connection requests per week to business owners in your target niche. After they accept, wait 3–5 days, then send a 2-sentence DM: "Hi [Name], I saw you're in [industry]. I've helped [X] businesses in your space increase online sales with better websites. Happy to chat if you're open to it."
  • Realistic timeline: LinkedIn is slow. Expect your first paid project in 6–8 weeks of consistent posting and outreach.

The advantage: warm conversations. Fewer rejections than cold email. The disadvantage: it takes time. Use this alongside lead lists for faster results.

3. Google Local Business (Google Maps) Outreach

Search for businesses in your city on Google Maps—restaurants, salons, real estate agencies, banks. Filter by industry. Visit their websites. If the site is ugly, outdated, or non-existent, they need you.

Call or WhatsApp the manager directly. Yes, really. Cold calling Nigerian business owners is not weird; it's expected. They respect directness.

Script:

"Hi [Name], I'm a web designer in [city]. I noticed your business on Google Maps. Your current website could be bringing in more customers if it were updated. I've worked with [X] similar businesses. Can we chat for 5 minutes?"

Close rate: 10–15% of calls lead to a conversation. 30–40% of conversations turn into projects. This is personal and works well for retail and services.

Pro tip: Call between 9 AM–11 AM or 3 PM–5 PM. Avoid lunch hours. Have a 30-second pitch ready.

4. Partner With Digital Marketing Agencies

Many digital marketing agencies in Nigeria (SEO, social media, branding) don't have in-house web designers. When clients ask for a website, agencies refer out. You can be their preferred web designer.

Approach 10–15 local agencies in your city. Offer a 10–15% referral fee per client they send you (they'll add it to your final invoice—clients don't mind). Make it easy: provide a one-pager with your portfolio and turnaround times.

Realistic: 2–3 agencies will actually send work your way. But 1–2 referrals per month adds up quickly.

5. WhatsApp Business & Community Groups

Nigerian entrepreneurs live in WhatsApp groups. Business owners, startup founders, e-commerce sellers, real estate agents—they're all in communities on WhatsApp.

Join 5–10 groups relevant to your target audience (search "Lagos Business Group," "Abuja Entrepreneurs," etc.). Post a simple intro: "I'm a web designer. I help online businesses build websites that convert. DM if interested."

Don't spam. Post once per week with a quick tip or case study. This is relationship building, not sales.

Conversion rate: Lower than cold email (0.5–1%), but the leads are warm because you're already in a trusted community.

6. Referral Network From Past Clients

This is passive income that compounds. After completing a project, ask your client: "Do you know 2–3 other business owners who might need a website?" Offer them ₦20,000–₦50,000 per referred client who signs.

Your past clients become sales reps. In 6–12 months, 30% of your new work comes from referrals. You're no longer hunting; clients come to you.

Real example: You design a website for a cosmetics brand (₦400,000 project). After launch, they refer two salon owners. You land both at ₦300,000 each. You pay the cosmetics brand ₦40,000 each. You made ₦520,000 net in 3 months from one relationship.

7. Retainer Contracts & Ongoing Relationships

One-off projects are feast-or-famine. Retainers are stability.

After building a website, pitch maintenance: "For ₦30,000/month, I'll handle updates, security, backups, and small changes. This keeps your site fast and safe."

Land 10 retainer clients at ₦30,000 each = ₦300,000 monthly passive income before any new projects.

The pitch: Most Nigerian business owners don't know their site needs maintenance. Educate them. Once they understand, retainers become easy upsells.

Cold Email Template for Nigerian Web Design Leads

Here's a template that works. (For more, see our guide on cold email templates.)

Subject: New website for [Business Name]?

Hi [Name],

I visited your website and noticed [specific observation: "it hasn't been updated since 2019" or "your contact form is broken"].

I've helped [X] similar businesses in [industry] redesign their sites, which increased inquiries by 40–60%.

Quick question: if a new website could bring you more customers, would that be worth a conversation?

[Your name]
[Phone + WhatsApp]

Why it works:

  • Specific (you visited their site—not a mass email).
  • Result-focused (mentions customer increase, not design trends).
  • Low-pressure (just a conversation).
  • Mobile-friendly (Nigerians read on phones; short and direct).

Once you have leads from Freelance LeadsHub, load them into Zaram Web Mailer and run 3–5 follow-ups over 2 weeks. Set the tool to avoid spam filters and handle unsubscribes automatically.

Common Mistakes (Avoid These)

  • No portfolio: Build 2–3 sample websites before cold outreach. Even if they're practice projects, they matter. Nigerian clients want proof you can deliver.
  • Vague pitches: Don't say "I build websites." Say "I build e-commerce sites that help retailers increase sales by 40–60%." Specificity closes deals.
  • Ignoring WhatsApp: Nigerian business owners prefer WhatsApp to email. Always include your WhatsApp number in outreach.
  • Too expensive too fast: Start at ₦200,000–₦400,000 per project. Once you have 10 testimonials, raise to ₦500,000+. Pricing grows with credibility.
  • No follow-up: Most "no" answers turn into "yes" with a second or third touch. Follow up 2–3 times before moving on.
  • Poor mobile experience: Your portfolio and emails must look perfect on phones. Most Nigerian prospects browse on 4G smartphones, not desktops.
