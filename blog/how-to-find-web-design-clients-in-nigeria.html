How to Find Web Design Clients in Nigeria (and Get Paid in Dollars) — 2025 Guide

July 2025 · 7 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

Web design is one of the best skills a Nigerian freelancer can have in 2025. Every business needs a website. Most small businesses in the US, UK, and Canada either have no website or have one that looks like it was built in 2011. And they are willing to pay — well — to fix that.

The problem most Nigerian web designers face is not skill. It is finding the right clients. They spend weeks on Upwork bidding on $50 jobs against 200 other proposals, or they post on LinkedIn and wait for something to happen. Neither strategy works consistently.

This guide covers the direct approach: how to find businesses that need a website, how to pitch them, how to price your work, and how to collect payment in dollars without any friction.

The Opportunity Nobody Is Talking About

Here are numbers that should change how you think about this:

30%+of US small businesses have no website
60%of consumers won't trust a business without a website
$350–$800average price for a 5-page small business site
3 clientsper month = ₦1.4M+ at current exchange rates

The businesses that need websites are sitting on Google Maps right now. They have phone numbers, email addresses, and reviews — but no functional website. They are not on Upwork looking for a web designer. They do not know there is a problem to solve. That is your job: to show up and tell them.

Who to Target: The Best Niches for Web Design Outreach

Not all businesses are equally easy to close. The best targets share three traits: they have a physical location, they depend on local customers finding them, and they have some money to spend. Here are the top niches:

Dental Clinics and Medical Practices

Doctors and dentists are very busy people who are not tech-savvy. They know they need a website but they never get around to sorting it out. They have high average revenue per patient, which means they can easily justify spending $400–$600 on a website. This is one of the easiest niches to close.

Law Firms and Attorneys

A solicitor with no website loses clients every day to competitors who rank on Google. Law firms are among the highest-paying web design clients — $600–$1,500 per site is normal because they understand ROI. One new client from a website pays for the site dozens of times over.

Restaurants and Cafés

A restaurant without a website has no online menu, no booking link, and no Google presence beyond a Maps listing. These are easy sells because the gap is obvious. Pitch at $300–$500 for a simple 4-page site with an online menu.

Gyms and Fitness Studios

The fitness industry went digital after COVID and never went back. Gym owners want class schedules, membership sign-ups, and a professional online presence. Easy pitch: "Your competitors have websites. You do not. Here is what that is costing you."

Beauty Salons and Spas

Salons depend almost entirely on local foot traffic and word of mouth. A website with photos, services, prices, and an online booking link can double their bookings. Pitch at $300–$500 and upsell monthly maintenance at $50–$80/month.

Real Estate Agents

Individual agents often do not have their own site — they rely on their agency's website. A personal brand site that showcases their listings and testimonials is a strong pitch. $400–$700 per site, with good conversion rates.

How to Find These Businesses With Emails

The old way: spend 3 hours manually searching Google Maps, clicking through business listings one by one, hoping to find an email address. You might collect 20 contacts in an afternoon.

The smart way: use a targeted lead list. You pick your niche (dentists), your country (USA), your state (Texas), and you get a CSV of hundreds or thousands of businesses with name, email, phone number, website URL, and Google Maps link. You open the file and start emailing.

💡 The difference between a freelancer who lands 2 clients a month and one who lands zero is almost always the size and quality of their lead list. You cannot pitch businesses you do not know exist.

When you have a list of 500 dentists in Texas, you can see immediately which ones have a website (filter by the website column) and which ones do not. The ones without websites are your warmest prospects — the problem is obvious, the pitch is easy.

How to Pitch: The Web Design Cold Email That Closes

Your pitch email should do one thing: make them curious enough to reply. Here is the structure:

  1. Subject line: "[Business Name] website" or "Quick question about [Business Name]"
  2. Opening: One line showing you looked at their specific business. "Found [Business Name] on Google Maps while looking for [niche] in [City] — couldn't find your website anywhere."
  3. The problem: State the consequence plainly. "Most people Google a business before calling. Without a site, you're probably losing 30–40% of potential customers to competitors."
  4. Your offer: Specific and low-risk. "I build clean, fast 5-page websites for [niche] businesses in 7–10 days, starting at $350. Here's one I did recently: [link]"
  5. The ask: A call, not a commitment. "Would a 15-minute call this week make sense?"

Keep the whole thing under 120 words. Attach nothing. One link to a portfolio example is fine.

How to Price Your Web Design Work in 2025

Nigerian web designers chronically underprice. If you are charging $50–$100 for a website, you are not being competitive — you are being exploited. Here is how to think about pricing:

Anchor to their revenue, not to your effort

A dentist earns $150–$300 per patient visit. If your website brings them 5 new patients per month, it pays for itself in the first week. Frame pricing in terms of what one new client is worth to them, not in terms of how many hours it takes you.

Package pricing (not hourly)

  • Starter site (3–4 pages, mobile-responsive): $300–$400
  • Business site (5–7 pages, contact form, Google Maps embed): $450–$650
  • Premium site (7+ pages, booking integration, SEO setup): $700–$1,000
  • Monthly maintenance (updates, hosting management, security): $50–$100/month

Collect 50% upfront, 50% on delivery

Never start work without a deposit. This is non-negotiable. A 50% upfront payment filters out time-wasters and ensures you get paid even if the client goes quiet mid-project.

How to Collect Payment in Dollars From Nigeria

This is simpler than most people think. Here are the best options right now:

Grey (grey.co)

Grey gives you a real US bank account (routing number + account number), a real UK sort code, and a Euro IBAN. You share these details with your client and they pay as if they were paying any US business. Grey converts to naira at near-market rates. This is the most seamless option for Nigerian freelancers right now.

Payoneer

Widely recognised internationally. You can send clients a Payoneer payment request directly. Withdrawal to Nigerian bank takes 1–3 days. Some US clients prefer this because it is familiar to them.

Wise

Excellent for UK and European clients. Lower fees than most alternatives. Transfer speeds are very fast.

USDT (Crypto)

Growing in popularity among Nigerian freelancers for speed and zero bank friction. Client sends USDT to your wallet, you sell on Binance P2P at market rate. Instant settlement, no chargebacks.

Quote everything in USD. Do not convert to naira in your proposal — it reads as unprofessional to international clients and you lose the psychological anchoring of dollar pricing.

Building a Portfolio When You Have No Clients Yet

Every web designer faces this: clients want to see work, but you need clients to have work to show. Here is how to break the cycle:

  • Build 2–3 spec sites. Pick a real local business with a bad website (a dentist in your city, a local restaurant) and rebuild their site as a concept. Show it in your portfolio as "Concept redesign — Bright Smile Dental, Lagos."
  • Offer a free site to a local business. One or two free sites in exchange for a testimonial and permission to show the work. After that, you charge.
  • Use a Behance or Dribbble profile. Even mockups and concept work displayed professionally look credible to foreign clients.
  • Build your own website first. Your own site is your first portfolio piece. If it looks good, clients will believe you can make theirs look good too.

The Numbers: What a Consistent Web Design Business Looks Like

Let us be concrete. If you send 30 cold emails per day and follow up properly:

  • 30 emails/day × 5 days = 150 emails/week
  • At 5% reply rate = 7–8 replies/week
  • At 25% close rate from replies = 1–2 clients/week
  • At $400 per site = $400–$800/week = $1,600–$3,200/month
  • At today's exchange rate = ₦2.5M–₦5M/month

These are conservative numbers. As you get better at writing emails and closing calls, conversion rates go up. Many Nigerian web designers running this model consistently close 3–5 clients per month working part-time.

The One Thing That Separates Freelancers Who Win From Those Who Don't

It is not skill. It is not even the quality of the pitch. It is volume and consistency. The freelancers earning well from web design in Nigeria right now started sending cold emails months ago — imperfect emails, rough pitches, prices they later raised — and they kept going.

Every email you do not send is a client you did not give yourself a chance at. Every follow-up you skip is a deal that quietly goes to someone else. The market is enormous. The competition in the cold email channel is almost zero because most people do not have the discipline to do it consistently.

You now have the method. The only variable left is whether you start today or wait another month.
