How to Get Your First Client as a Freelancer in Nigeria: A Practical 30-Day Plan

17 July 2026 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

Your first client as a freelancer in Nigeria is the hardest to get. Not because you're not skilled—but because you're competing with thousands of other freelancers on Upwork and Fiverr, undercutting each other, and fighting for scraps.

The truth: most Nigerian freelancers never land their first paying client. They spend weeks building a Fiverr profile, posting 50 gigs, and getting nothing. Then they quit.

But there's a faster way. This guide shows you exactly how to get your first client as a freelancer in Nigeria—without relying on crowded platforms, without underpricing yourself, and without waiting months. You can have your first paying client and first deposit in Payoneer or Wise within 30 days if you execute this plan.

Why You're Not Getting Clients (And What Actually Works)

Before we get into the system, let's be honest about why you haven't landed a client yet.

The Upwork/Fiverr Trap

On Upwork, you're competing with 500+ freelancers on every job. Clients sort by price, not quality. A freelancer in Pakistan quotes $5/hour. You quote $15/hour. You lose. This is not a business model—it's a race to the bottom.

Fiverr is even worse. You're locked into their platform, their algorithm, their 20% commission. Your earnings get stuck in conversion rates (Fiverr pays out in USD, you need it in NGN, and the conversion takes days).

What Actually Works: Direct Outreach to Real Businesses

The freelancers making ₦500,000+ per month in Nigeria aren't grinding Upwork. They're selling directly to business owners who:

  • Have money to spend (not browsing freelance marketplaces looking for the cheapest option)
  • Need work done urgently (they're not posting a job and waiting for 100 bids)
  • Pay faster (direct bank transfers, no platform middleman)
  • Come back for repeat work (one client becomes ₦2–5M/month in recurring revenue)

The catch? You have to find them first. And most freelancers don't know how.

Key insight: Your first client will likely come from direct outreach, not a platform. A single direct client at ₦150,000–300,000 per project is worth 100 Fiverr gigs at ₦5,000 each.

The 30-Day Plan to Land Your First Client

Week 1: Preparation (Days 1–7)

Step 1: Define Your Niche and Target Client

You need to be specific. "I'm a freelance writer" won't work. "I write product descriptions for ecommerce stores" will.

Pick one of these based on your skills:

  • Web Design/Development: Target small businesses, ecommerce stores, agencies needing dev work
  • Content Writing: Target blogs, SaaS companies, ecommerce product pages
  • Virtual Assistance: Target agencies, coaches, business owners (these pay ₦80,000–200,000/month)
  • Graphics/Branding: Target startups, ecommerce, digital marketing agencies
  • Social Media Management: Target small businesses, personal brands, local services (salons, restaurants)

Write down your answer: "I help [specific business type] with [specific problem]."

Example: "I help ecommerce stores in Nigeria increase conversion rates through professional product photography and descriptions."

Step 2: Build a One-Page Freelance Pitch/Portfolio

You don't need a fancy website. Use Google Docs, Canva, or a simple Carrd.co page. It should have:

  • Your name and what you do (3 sentences max)
  • 3–5 examples of your best work (use previous projects, volunteer work, or portfolio pieces you've created)
  • Your rates (in USD or GBP, not NGN—clients respect that more; example: $300–800 for a project)
  • Client testimonials (ask previous clients or people you've helped for one-line reviews)
  • Your contact info (email and WhatsApp)

This takes 2–3 hours. Don't overthink it. Done is better than perfect.

Step 3: Set Up Payment Infrastructure

Nigerian clients will ask: "How do I pay you?" You need to be ready.

Open accounts on (if you don't have them already):

  • Payoneer — Accepts payments from clients worldwide, converts to NGN, deposits to your Nigerian bank account
  • Wise (formerly TransferWise) — Better rates than Payoneer for USD to NGN conversion; can receive payments directly
  • Stripe or Flutterwave — If you want to accept card payments

At current rates (July 2026), 1 USD ≈ 1,500–1,600 NGN on Payoneer and Wise. A $500 project = ₦750,000–800,000. A $1,000 project = ₦1.5–1.6M.

Get your account details ready so you can send them to clients in under 1 minute.

Step 4: Get a Targeted Lead List

You could manually Google for hours. Or you could use Freelance LeadsHub.

Instead of spending 20 hours searching for leads, you spend 20 minutes downloading them. This alone cuts your time-to-first-client in half.

Get a lead list of 100–200 businesses in your niche. Cost: usually ₦5,000–15,000 for a list that would take you 40+ hours to compile manually.

Step 5: Qualify and Sort Your Leads

Not all leads are equal. Spend 30 minutes sorting:

  • Tier 1 (Hot leads): Businesses that clearly need your service right now. Example: ecommerce stores with outdated websites, no product descriptions, or poor social media presence.
  • Tier 2 (Warm leads): Businesses that might need your service. Reach out after Tier 1.
  • Tier 3 (Cold leads): Long shots. Try these if you have time.

Focus on Tier 1. You're looking for quick wins, not perfect targeting.

Week 3: Outreach and Pitching (Days 15–21)

Step 6: Write Your Cold Email Template

Your email needs to be short (under 150 words), personal, and focused on THEIR problem, not your service.

Template:

Subject: Quick question about [their business name]

Hi [Name],

I noticed [specific observation about their business]. A lot of [type of business] struggle with [specific problem], which usually costs them [specific cost/loss].

I help [business type] solve this by [brief solution]. We typically see [specific result] within [timeframe].

Would be worth a quick call to explore. Free, no obligation.

Best,
[Your name]
[Phone + WhatsApp]

See our cold email templates for more examples specific to different services.

Step 7: Send Cold Emails at Scale

Once you have your lead list and email template, you need to send to 50–100 leads. Sending manually will take 5+ hours and get boring.

Use Zaram Web Mailer to automate this. It's built for Nigerian freelancers and handles:

  • Bulk email sending (send to 100 leads in 2 minutes)
  • Personalization (auto-fills names, company details)
  • Avoiding spam filters (most emails land in inbox, not spam)
  • Follow-up sequences (sends reminder emails to non-responders after 3–5 days)

Load your lead list into Zaram Web Mailer, set it to send 20–30 emails per day (avoids looking like spam), and let it work.

In one week, you'll have sent 100+ personalized emails. Expect a 2–5% response rate (2–5 replies from 100 emails). That's your qualified leads.

Step 8: Follow Up on Responses

When someone replies, respond within 1 hour. They're interested right now.

Keep it short:

Thanks for getting back to me! Happy to help. Quick question: what's your biggest challenge with [their problem area] right now? I can give you specific advice in our call.

Free 20-min call this week? You can grab a time here [Calendly link] or I'm free Wed 2–4pm Lagos time on WhatsApp.

Most will either ignore you (they got busy) or book a call. Book 3–5 calls. One will convert.

Week 4: Closing Your First Client (Days 22–30)

Step 9: The Sales Call

You have 20 minutes. Your goal is not to sell. It's to understand their problem and see if you can help.

Structure:

  • First 2 min: Small talk, rapport
  • Next 10 min: Ask questions. "Tell me about your business. What's your biggest challenge right now? What have you tried?"
  • Next 5 min: Explain your solution (only if it fits their problem)
  • Last 3 min: Suggest next steps. "Here's what I'd do... my rate is $X. Sound good?"

If they say yes, send a proposal within 1 hour.

Step 10: Send a Proposal

One page. Include:

  • What you'll deliver (specific, detailed)
  • Timeline (realistic; add buffer)
  • Cost (in USD, not NGN; example: $500–1,500 depending on scope)
  • Payment terms (50% upfront, 50% on completion is standard)
  • How they pay (Payoneer link, Wise details, bank transfer—give options)

Send it as a PDF. Ask for a yes/no by a specific date (3 days out).

Step 11: Close and Deliver

They say yes. Money comes in (50% upfront). Now you deliver.

Deliver early, over-deliver in quality, ask for a testimonial.

Boom. You have your first client and your first payment in Payoneer or Wise.

Real Numbers: What You Can Actually Earn

Let's be concrete. Here's what Nigerian freelancers typically earn on their first client:

  • Content writer: $200–500 per article (₦300,000–750,000) or ₦50,000–100,000/month for retainer work
  • Web designer: $500–2,000 per site (₦750,000–3M) or ₦200,000–500,000 for ongoing maintenance
  • Virtual assistant: $200–400/month (₦300,000–600,000/month) for part-time work
  • Social media manager: $150–500/month (₦225,000–750,000/month)
  • Graphic designer: $100–500 per project (₦150,000–750,000)

Your first client might only pay $300 (₦450,000). That's fine. It's proof of concept. Client #2 will pay more. By client #5–10, you're at ₦500,000–1M/month.

The money comes from repetition and direct clients, not from one big deal.

The Role of Direct Outreach vs. Platforms

We said platforms don't work. That's not entirely true—they work, just slowly and at lower rates.

The hybrid approach:

  • Months 1–3: Cold outreach only (get your first 3–5 clients). This is fastest.
  • Months 4+: Mix of cold outreach (new clients) and referrals (clients refer you). Platforms become optional.

Referrals are your best acquisition channel. A happy first client tells 2–3 other businesses about you. That's free marketing.

Once you have 2–3 clients, word spreads. You stop needing cold email. But to get those first 2–3 clients, cold outreach is the only reliable method.

Pro tip: After your first client, ask them: "Who else should I talk to?" That referral is warmer than any cold email and has a 40%+ close rate.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Trying to Serve Everyone

"I'm a web developer, content writer, and graphic designer." Wrong. Pick one. Master it. Earn ₦1M/month. Then expand.

Mistake 2: Underpricing to Get Your First Client

Don't charge $100 for a $500 project just to "get experience." You're teaching clients to expect low prices. Charge fair rates (research your niche), deliver excellent work. The first client should set the tone.

Mistake 3: Waiting for Perfection

Your portfolio doesn't need to be perfect. Send the first email with 80% done. Perfectionism kills momentum.

Mistake 4: Not Following Up

Send one email, get no reply, move on. Wrong. Send 3–5 follow-ups over 2 weeks. Most deals close on follow-up #3.

Mistake 5: Ignoring Payment Setup

Client says yes, wants to pay, but you take 5 days to send bank details. They change their mind. Have payment ready before you pitch.

What About Referrals and Networking in Nigeria?

Referrals are powerful. But you need your first client before referrals work. You can't ask for a referral from someone who hasn't hired you.

Cold outreach is your bootstrap method. Referrals are your scaling method.

Once you have your first 3 clients, attend 2–3 industry events, join WhatsApp groups, post on LinkedIn. By then, you have social proof ("I helped X business do Y"), so networking works.

But the path is: cold email → first client → referrals → word of mouth → referral-only growth.

Data Costs and NEPA: Nigerian-Specific Challenges

We know: Nigerian internet is expensive (NEPA, airtime, data). Budget for this:

  • Daily data: ₦500–1,000 (via MTN/Airtel)
  • Monthly backup (WiFi or electricity): ₦5,000–15,000
  • Laptop/power bank: One-time cost, but essential

Total ongoing cost for staying online: ₦10,000–20,000/month.

When your first client pays ₦300,000+, this cost becomes invisible. But in the meantime, budget for it.
