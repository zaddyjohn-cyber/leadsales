How to Become a Copywriter in Nigeria and Earn in Dollars: A Step-by-Step Guide

2026-07-24 · 9 min read · By Freelance LeadsHub

Copywriting is one of the fastest ways to start earning dollars as a Nigerian freelancer. Unlike web design, app development, or other technical skills, copywriting has a low barrier to entry—you don't need expensive equipment or years of formal training. What you need is clarity, persistence, and the right clients.

The market demand is real. American and UK businesses constantly need writers who can:

  • Write sales emails that convert
  • Create product descriptions that sell
  • Write landing page copy
  • Craft ad copy for Facebook, Google, and LinkedIn
  • Write email sequences for marketing automation

And they'll pay between $500–$5,000 USD per project. At current exchange rates (₦1,500 to ₦1,700 per USD), that's ₦750,000 to ₦8.5 million naira per project. This guide shows you exactly how to become a copywriter in Nigeria and earn in dollars—without competing on Upwork where rates are cut-throat.

Step 1: Master the Fundamentals (4–8 Weeks)

You don't need a degree in English. You need to understand how copywriting works—which is the inverse of what most Nigerians learn in school.

What Traditional Education Gets Wrong

School teaches you to write essays. Copywriting is about writing for a specific outcome: a click, a sale, a response, a signup. The best copywriter isn't the one with the most eloquent prose—it's the one who moves the reader to action.

The Core Skills You Actually Need

  1. Understanding your audience's pain: Before you write a single word, you must know who you're writing for and what keeps them awake at night.
  2. The AIDA framework: Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. Most weak copy skips the first three and jumps to a demand.
  3. Headline writing: 80% of readers only read the headline. If it doesn't grab them, the body copy doesn't matter.
  4. Calls to action (CTAs): "Buy now" is weak. "Lock in your discount for the next 24 hours" is strong.
  5. Objection handling: Good copy doesn't hide the downsides—it addresses them head-on.

Key Insight: Nigerian copywriters often make the mistake of writing "British English formal" copy. The best-converting copy is conversational, clear, and direct. Write like you talk to a friend, not like you're writing an exam essay.

Where to Learn (Free and Cheap Resources)

  • Swipe files: Go to ConvertKit.com, Substack.com, and note how successful creators write their sales pages and emails. This is free research.
  • Books: Cashvertising by Drew Eric Whitman (₦5,000–₦8,000 on Jumia) teaches psychological triggers. The Copywriter's Handbook by Robert Bly is the industry standard.
  • YouTube: Search "copywriting tutorials" or "email copywriting." Most foundational content is free. Channels like Copyhackers (though paid) have free clips.
  • Practice: Write 5 different headlines for a product you use every day. Write 3 versions of a sales email. This costs ₦0.

Step 2: Build a Portfolio (Real Work, Not Fake)

Clients will ask: "What have you written before?" If you say "nothing," you won't land high-paying jobs. But you don't need to take unpaid gigs for months.

Create Real-World Portfolio Pieces

  • Write for a small Nigerian business for free—once: Find a local Lagos restaurant, fashion brand, or service on Instagram. Offer to rewrite their product descriptions or write a sales email for their launch. Get 2–3 of these done. Total time: 2–3 weeks.
  • Write for your own micro-niche: If you like fitness, write a mini sales funnel (3 emails) selling a fitness eBook you create. You're not selling it—you're proving you can write a complete sales sequence.
  • Deconstruct and rewrite: Take a famous sales page (like ConvertKit's pricing page). Rewrite it better. Include it in your portfolio as "alternative version."

A portfolio doesn't need 20 pieces. 3–5 strong pieces showing different formats (email, landing page, product description, ad copy) is enough.

Step 3: Choose Your Niche (Critical)

Generalist copywriters earn $500–$1,500 per project. Niche copywriters earn $2,000–$5,000+ per project. Why? Because they understand the specific pain of their audience.

High-Paying Niches for Nigerian Copywriters

  • SaaS (Software as a Service): Businesses selling software pay the most. They know copywriting directly impacts revenue.
  • E-commerce and supplements: High-ticket products = high budgets for copy.
  • Financial services and crypto: Complex products need clear explanation. They pay well.
  • Coaching and course creators: Personal brands and online educators need sales funnels and email sequences constantly.
  • B2B tech: Writing copy for businesses selling to other businesses. High-value deals = high copy budgets.

Pick ONE. Spend the next 2–3 months becoming expert-level in that niche. Read case studies. Join forums. Understand the lingo. Follow 10 successful businesses in that space on LinkedIn.

Step 4: Get Your First Dollar-Paying Clients (The Direct Outreach Method)

This is where most Nigerian freelancers fail. They wait for clients to find them. They don't.

Why Upwork and Fiverr Don't Work for Copywriting

On Upwork, you're competing against 50,000 other freelancers. Clients sort by price. A client looking for "email copywriting" will see someone from Bangladesh offering the same work for $50. You can't compete on price and maintain your sanity.

The solution? Go directly to businesses. Most small and mid-sized companies don't even look at freelancing platforms. They get their copywriters through:

  • Direct outreach (cold email)
  • Referrals
  • Agencies

The Cold Email Strategy That Works

Step 1: Get a targeted lead list. Go to Freelance LeadsHub and download businesses in your niche (e.g., "SaaS companies in the US" or "e-commerce store owners"). You get their name, email, phone, and website. This costs ₦2,000–₦5,000 for a list of 500 leads depending on the niche.

Step 2: Write a short, specific cold email. Your email should:

  • Reference something specific from their website (not "Hi [Name], I'm a copywriter")
  • Show one example of similar work you've done
  • Make ONE offer (don't ask for a call, don't ask for their rate)
  • Keep it to 75 words max

Example cold email:

Hi [Name],

I noticed your product page explains features but doesn't show the transformation. I rewrote your landing page copy and got 32% higher CTR on the CTA button.

I help SaaS companies convert more visitors into trials. I write the email sequences, landing pages, and ad copy.

Want me to audit your current copy and suggest 3 quick wins?

—[Your name]

At a 5% response rate, 500 leads = 25 interested prospects. Of those, 20–30% will book a call. That's 5–7 calls. Of those, 20–40% become clients.

Step 5: Pricing and Positioning

What to Charge

Never charge hourly as a copywriter. Clients don't buy hours—they buy results.

Starting rates (Month 1–3):

  • One-off sales email: $300–$500
  • Product description (5–10 items): $500–$800
  • Landing page copy: $800–$1,500
  • Full email sequence (5 emails): $1,200–$2,000

After 3–6 months (with case studies):

  • Landing page: $1,500–$2,500
  • Email sequence: $2,000–$3,500
  • Full funnel (landing page + 5 emails + ads): $3,000–$6,000

Why these prices? A $2,000 sales email that increases conversions by 20% saves a client thousands. They'll pay because you're not a cost—you're an investment that returns 10x.

How to Present Your Rates

Don't lead with price. Lead with value:

"I charge $1,500 for a landing page. Most clients see a 15–25% lift in conversion within 2 weeks of launch."

See the difference? You're not saying "I'm expensive." You're saying "I deliver ROI."

Step 6: Keep Learning and Raising Rates

After your first 10 projects, you'll notice patterns. Some clients are easier to work with. Some projects are more lucrative. Some copy-types you're better at.

Every 3 months:

  • Raise your rates by 10–15%
  • Drop clients who don't respect your time
  • Specialize further (e.g., "I write email sequences for e-commerce brands")
  • Study what your top clients are doing

The copywriters earning $10,000+ per month didn't start there. They started at $300 per project and raised rates systematically as they proved results.

Step 7: Logistics—Getting Paid in Dollars

You earn dollars, but you're in Nigeria. Here's what actually works:

Payment Methods

  • Wise (formerly TransferWise): Clients pay your Wise account. You keep it there or transfer to your Nigerian bank account at true market rate. Wise is trusted, fast, and costs ₦1,000–₦2,000 per transfer.
  • Payoneer: Similar to Wise. Many US clients know it. Withdrawal to Nigerian banks is straightforward.
  • Grey (Remitano): Convert USD to NGN peer-to-peer. Good rates but requires KYC.
  • Direct bank transfer: Ask clients for a direct wire. This works if they have international accounts set up.

Open a Wise account first. It takes 15 minutes online, requires a valid Nigerian ID, and zero bank account balance.
